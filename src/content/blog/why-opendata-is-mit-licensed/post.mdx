## OpenData and the license question

The state of open source databases, from where we sit, looks like unfinished business. Each category (relational, document, key-value, timeseries, search, vector, streaming) has its own battle-hardened projects, and each one is also its own ops commitment. Operating several of them means running several distinct stateful storage systems, each with its own storage model, failure modes, and tuning surface. The practical alternative most teams pick is paying a cloud vendor to do it for them, at prices that are often punitive. OpenData is our attempt at a better shape: a family of open source databases built on a shared, object-store-native foundation, so that once a team knows how to operate one, they already know most of how to operate the next. The [project docs](https://www.opendata.dev/docs) and our [manifesto](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/manifesto) lay out what we are building and why.

We launched [OpenData Timeseries](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/introducing-timeseries) last week, the first public database in the family. Every OpenData database is MIT-licensed, and the choice prompted two kinds of reactions. Some readers were grateful we picked a genuinely permissive license. Others were skeptical: why not AGPL, and aren't we worried about a large cloud provider taking the code and running a managed service on top of it?

The common framing behind the skeptical reaction is that a permissive license trades business durability for adoption: you get more users in exchange for less protection against someone else eating your lunch. We don't think that framing holds, at least not for databases. Building an independent database business is hard regardless of which license you pick, and a restrictive license does not change the economics in your favor the way people assume. It mostly just slows adoption today. The choice isn't a hard one.

## Why open source is the right vehicle for OpenData

Plenty of people have made the general case for open source infrastructure. Eric Raymond's [The Cathedral and the Bazaar](http://www.catb.org/~esr/writings/cathedral-bazaar/cathedral-bazaar/) is still the most durable version of it. The short version is that a permissive license removes friction from adoption: a developer at a large company can evaluate OpenData, ship it inside a product, and send patches back without routing anything through legal, and a solo developer can drop it into a side project without worrying about license compatibility. In the early years of a project, the difference between low-friction and high-friction licensing is the difference between adoption starting and adoption stalling.

The specific case for OpenData has two pieces. The first is that good databases cannot be built without deep, real-world feedback from operators. Databases are operational systems above all else, and the ways they fail or surprise you at scale are impossible to fully enumerate from a startup's test environment. Open source is the most direct path to that feedback: developers can run OpenData on their own workloads with zero procurement risk, report back concretely on what works and what doesn't, and in many cases contribute the fix themselves.

The second is that OpenData's object-store-native architecture is still early: most database categories have not been rebuilt this way yet, and the operational and cost properties it makes possible are still being discovered. A lot of what will make OpenData valuable over time is going to come from someone pushing it into a use case we did not anticipate. That kind of bottom-up direction-setting happens far more often in projects that are fully open than in ones where the license keeps users at a distance. Because every database in the family shares a storage foundation, a capability surfaced in one OpenData system tends to carry naturally into the rest.

Our intention over time is to move the OpenData projects into a neutral foundation, so that the license guarantee is backed by governance that outlasts our own company. No single entity, including ours, should stand between an OpenData user and their right to use the software. Being unambiguous about that intent now is how we start earning the trust a community needs.

## Proprietary database IP does not last

The usual argument for a more restrictive license is protection: against being copied by a competitor, against a cloud provider running a managed service on the code, against the general worry that someone else will extract more value from the project than we will. These are reasonable concerns to raise, and we do not dismiss them. But in the database world, that protection has always been thinner than people believe, and AI coding tools are making it thinner still.

Databases are expensive to build and maintain, but like all software, they are cheap to copy. The novel insight that differentiates a database is usually the thing the vendor markets hardest, because marketing is how the vendor wins deals. Once an insight is described in talks, papers, and docs, re-implementing it is straightforward. What used to take a competitor years now takes weeks with a competent engineer and a coding agent. My co-founder Almog laid this out in more detail in [The Broken Economics of Databases](https://www.bitsxpages.com/p/the-broken-economics-of-databases), but the short version is that every database vendor eventually faces margin pressure from look-alike products. Vendors with an open source core see it earlier and more acutely. Vendors with a proprietary core are not immune; they just get a delay.

Very few database companies stay independent over the long run, and those that do have all diversified:

- Oracle moved up the stack into applications (PeopleSoft, Siebel, NetSuite) and down into hardware and cloud infrastructure (Sun, OCI).
- IBM folded Db2 into a broader software-and-services business, and absorbed Informix and Netezza along the way.
- Sybase ended up inside SAP, and Teradata is still independent but not the business it was fifteen years ago.
- MongoDB grew from a document database into a broader platform layering in full-text search, vector search, and stream processing.
- Elastic did the same from the opposite direction, starting with search and expanding into observability, security analytics, and vector capabilities.
- Redis wrapped its core key-value store in a growing set of modules covering full-text search, JSON, time series, and vector indexing.

In every case, the company had to keep adding adjacent database categories to stay viable, because the original database alone was not enough.

Hamilton Helmer's [7 Powers](https://7powers.com/) frames why: persistent differential returns come from one of a handful of structural properties (scale economies, network economies, switching costs, counter-positioning, and a few others), and very few database companies have any of them. Steve Ballmer made the same point [on Acquired](https://youtu.be/CYC49_aeop0?si=c_JjVNX8ntzH9GPw&t=5388) when he called most businesses in history zero-trick ponies. Most database companies, judged honestly, are zero-trick ponies. The ones we remember as lasting independent businesses used a database as a beachhead for something more defensible.

Put this way, the tradeoff the skeptics raise is not really a tradeoff. A restrictive license buys us very little protective upside in the long term, because the ideas behind OpenData will be copied regardless. What it does cost us is adoption today, especially early on, when a copyleft obligation or an ambiguous CLA is enough to stall a developer before they try the thing. The honest trade on offer from a restrictive license is less adoption in exchange for protection the market is busy erasing anyway, and that is not a trade we have any interest in making.

## Building a database business in 2026

Given all of that, the real work is in the company we build around the code. Building one that can keep going under the pressures every database company faces has gotten harder, not easier. Open source database companies have been getting monetization wrong for a decade and a half, and AI is compressing the copying cycle that already punished them. Our approach has two parts.

The first is not to assume moats we do not have. Everything we do will be copied, and a cloud hyperscaler could, in principle, take OpenData and run a managed service on top of it. The failure mode most visible in the recent history of this space is not getting copied; it is growing a company that *requires* durable 70-80% gross margins and *requires* compounding growth to keep going. If a business has defensible moats, that shape of growth can work. If it does not and pretends otherwise, the inevitable result is a relicense, an acquisition, or a painful retreat a few years later, and whoever built on the old terms ends up carrying the cost. We would rather keep the team small for as long as that works, so we can take opportunities as they come instead of forcing growth to defend margins we were never entitled to. Modern AI tooling makes this posture more sustainable than it would have been five years ago: a small, senior team can now operate at a scope that used to require several times as many people, which widens the set of business shapes that stay viable without assumed moats.

The second is that OpenData has a structural advantage the single-database companies we learn from did not have, and we intend to use it. Because the family shares a storage foundation, cross-database work compounds on both the technical and the business side. Operators adopting one OpenData database have a short path to adopting the next one, because the operational model is already familiar. Tooling, integrations, and operational muscle built around one database carry into the rest. A managed offering over the family gets scope economies that a single-database managed offering cannot. It is too early to commit to specific business shapes, and we are deliberately not doing so before we have earned the right to. A multi-database footprint changes the base of what is possible in a way single-database predecessors did not have available. The kind of diversification MongoDB, Elastic, and Redis had to retrofit under market pressure is built into the shape of OpenData from the start.

We believe OpenData is a genuine contribution to the database space. MIT is the most direct path to making it useful to as many people as possible, and the community is the first step toward making it durable. If you have been watching OpenData and want to contribute, the license is our commitment that your work will stay yours to use.
