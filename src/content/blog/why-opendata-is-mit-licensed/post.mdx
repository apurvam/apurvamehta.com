## The license question

OpenData is a collection of open source databases built on a common storage engine using [SlateDB](http://www.slatedb.io) and object storage. The [project docs](https://www.opendata.dev/docs) and our [manifesto](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/manifesto) explain what we are building and why. Every database in the OpenData family is MIT-licensed, and the choice was deliberate.

When we launched [OpenData Timeseries last week](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/introducing-timeseries), two reactions kept showing up in comments. One was gratitude that we had picked a genuinely permissive license. The other was skepticism: why not AGPL, and aren't we worried about a large cloud provider taking the code and running a managed service on top of it?

Both reactions are fair, and both point at the same thing. Our short answer is that a restrictive license would not protect us from what developers worry about, and the real risks to OpenData live in the company we build around the code, not in the license.

## Proprietary database IP does not last

The case for a restrictive license rests on a premise that is worth challenging: that your core database IP is something worth protecting in the first place. In the database category, it almost always is not.

Databases are expensive to build and maintain, but like all software, they are cheap to copy. The novel insight that differentiates a database is typically also the thing the vendor markets hardest, because marketing is how the vendor wins deals. Once the insight is well described in talks, papers, and docs, re-implementing it is straightforward. AI tooling is accelerating this dynamic further; the gap between a published idea and a working clone shrinks every quarter.

My co-founder Almog laid this out in more detail in [The Broken Economics of Databases](https://www.bitsxpages.com/p/the-broken-economics-of-databases), but the short version is that every database vendor eventually faces margin pressure from look-alike products. Vendors with an open source core see it earlier and more acutely. Vendors with a proprietary core are not immune; they just get a delay.

This is why so few database companies survive as independent database companies. The ones that do have diversified. Oracle built its sustainable business by moving up the stack into applications (PeopleSoft, Siebel, NetSuite) and down the stack into hardware and cloud infrastructure (Sun, OCI). IBM folded Db2 into a broader software-and-services business and absorbed Informix and Netezza along the way. Sybase ended up inside SAP. Teradata is still independent, but it is not the business it was fifteen years ago. More recently, Elastic, Redis, MongoDB, and CockroachDB have each either relicensed their source or pivoted hard toward a managed service, because the pure-play open source database business was not generating enough margin on its own.

Hamilton Helmer's [7 Powers](https://7powers.com/) gives a useful frame here. It describes the properties that let a business generate persistent differential returns: scale economies, network economies, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resources, and process power. Very few database companies have any of them. Steve Ballmer used a similar frame [on Acquired](https://youtu.be/CYC49_aeop0?si=c_JjVNX8ntzH9GPw&t=5388), where he talked about businesses as zero, one, or two-trick ponies, and observed that almost every business in history is a zero-trick pony.

Most database companies, judged honestly, are zero-trick ponies. They have high fixed costs, because database engineers and enterprise sellers are expensive, and they struggle to recoup those costs because their edge is competed away on a timeline they cannot control. The ones we remember as independent successes are the ones that used a database as a beachhead for something more defensible.

Given that, pretending a restrictive license gives us real protection would be a form of self-deception. The ideas behind OpenData will be copied regardless of our license; what restrictive licensing actually does is limit who can use our software today, which is the opposite of what we want.

## Why MIT is the right choice for OpenData

Since restrictive licensing does not give us durable protection, OpenData's real edge has to come from somewhere else: a community that adopts and improves the code.

Open source databases get better when the community around them grows. More users surface edge cases, contributors improve operability and compatibility, integrations pull the system into new stacks, and operators pressure-test the design under load patterns we would never see ourselves. That flywheel is what turns a promising database into a battle-hardened one, and it is slow to start. Anything that adds friction at the outset (license ambiguity, CLA surprises, copyleft obligations a company's legal team has to review) bleeds momentum at exactly the moment it is most fragile.

MIT is the lowest-friction license we could pick. A developer at a large company can evaluate OpenData, fork it, ship it inside a product, and contribute patches back without a lawyer in the loop. Solo developers can drop it into a side project without thinking about license compatibility. And when a cloud provider hosts it commercially, which we know worries some people, that is actually evidence the project is succeeding. Our intention over time is to move the OpenData projects into a neutral foundation, so the license is backed by a governance structure that makes those guarantees durable beyond our own company.

The permissive license and the foundation path are how we signal to the ecosystem that we are serious about building a community rather than extracting from one. That signal matters because developers have watched enough projects make the opposite move that skepticism is rational. Being unambiguous about this up front is what earns the trust the flywheel needs to start turning.

## Risks

The risks are real. A large cloud provider could fork OpenData, run it as a managed service, and capture most of the revenue that a thriving community generates. A well-funded competitor could take the code and outspend us on go-to-market. These are the exact scenarios that motivated the license changes at Elastic, Redis, MongoDB, and CockroachDB, and we do not dismiss them.

Our approach to these risks is not to tighten the license but to be honest about what kind of company we are building. A thriving open source database can support many sustainable businesses at once. A larger company can build enterprise extensions or a polished managed developer experience on top of the core. A smaller consultancy can specialize in a vertical and serve a handful of deep customers well. Plenty of options exist between those two extremes. The failure mode is growing the company beyond what the business can actually support, whatever shape the business takes.

The recent history of open source database companies is full of teams that raised large rounds on the assumption of durable high margins, scaled their headcount and GTM to match those margins, and then had to relicense or restructure when the margins compressed. The real risk we are planning around is the company-building choices that follow from misreading the market's unit economics, not the license itself. If we grow responsibly (keeping the team lean for as long as it makes sense) and build a business whose economics do not depend on a moat we do not actually have, the license question stops mattering.

We believe the [OpenData](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/manifesto) idea is a genuine contribution to the database space. The MIT license is the most direct path to making that contribution useful to as many people as possible, and building the community is the first step toward making it durable. If you have been watching OpenData and want to contribute, the license is our commitment that your work will stay yours to use.
