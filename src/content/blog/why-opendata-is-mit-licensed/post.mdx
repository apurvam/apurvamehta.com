## The license question

OpenData is a family of open source databases that share a storage engine built on [SlateDB](http://www.slatedb.io) and object storage. Every database in the family inherits the same operational primitives: storage, durability, tuning surface, cost model. The intent is that once a developer knows how to run one OpenData database, they already know how to run the next. The [project docs](https://www.opendata.dev/docs) and our [manifesto](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/manifesto) cover what we are building and why.

Every database in the OpenData family is MIT-licensed, and the choice was deliberate. When we launched [OpenData Timeseries last week](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/introducing-timeseries), that choice produced two different reactions in the comments. One was gratitude that we had picked a genuinely permissive license. The other was skepticism: why not AGPL, and aren't we worried about a large cloud provider taking the code and running a managed service on top of it?

Both reactions assume the license is where durability lives. We don't think it is. The company we build around the code matters more to OpenData's long-term durability than the license text does, and MIT is the license that gives that company the best shot. A more restrictive license would not help us build the right company; it would just slow adoption today.

## Proprietary database IP does not last

Databases are expensive to build and maintain, but like all software, they are cheap to copy. The novel insight that differentiates a database is typically also the thing the vendor markets hardest, because marketing is how the vendor wins deals. Once the insight is well described in talks, papers, and docs, re-implementing it is straightforward. AI coding agents have accelerated this dynamic to Mach 10: what used to take a competitor years to re-implement now takes weeks.

My co-founder Almog laid this out in more detail in [The Broken Economics of Databases](https://www.bitsxpages.com/p/the-broken-economics-of-databases), but the short version is that every database vendor eventually faces margin pressure from look-alike products. Vendors with an open source core see it earlier and more acutely. Vendors with a proprietary core are not immune; they just get a delay.

This is why so few database companies stay independent over the long run. The ones that do have diversified. Oracle built its sustainable business by moving up the stack into applications (PeopleSoft, Siebel, NetSuite) and down into hardware and cloud infrastructure (Sun, OCI). IBM folded Db2 into a broader software-and-services business and absorbed Informix and Netezza along the way. Sybase ended up inside SAP. Teradata is still independent, but it is not the business it was fifteen years ago. More recently, MongoDB has grown from a document database into a broader platform that layers in full-text search, vector search, and stream processing. Elastic has done the same from the opposite direction, starting with search and expanding into observability, security analytics, and vector capabilities. Redis has wrapped its core key-value store in a growing set of modules covering full-text search, JSON, time series, and vector indexing. In every case, the company had to keep adding adjacent database categories to stay viable, because the original database alone was not enough.

Hamilton Helmer's [7 Powers](https://7powers.com/) gives a useful frame here. It describes the properties that let a business generate persistent differential returns: scale economies, network economies, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resources, and process power. Very few database companies have any of them. Steve Ballmer used a similar frame [on Acquired](https://youtu.be/CYC49_aeop0?si=c_JjVNX8ntzH9GPw&t=5388), where he talked about businesses as zero, one, or two-trick ponies, and observed that almost every business in history is a zero-trick pony.

Most database companies, judged honestly, are zero-trick ponies. They have high fixed costs, because database engineers and enterprise sellers are expensive, and they struggle to recoup those costs because competitors erode their edge on a timeline they cannot control. The ones we remember as independent successes are the ones that used a database as a beachhead for something more defensible.

Given that, pretending a restrictive license gives us real protection is self-deception. The ideas behind OpenData will get copied regardless of our license. A restrictive one does not stop that; it just narrows who can use our software today, which is the opposite of what we want.

## Why MIT is the right choice for OpenData

If the IP itself is not the moat, then a restrictive license is not protecting anything valuable. It is just limiting how many developers can use OpenData today. Our goal is to make a genuine contribution to the database world. Broad adoption is the most direct path there, and a permissive license is the most direct tool for broad adoption.

MIT is the lowest-friction license we could have picked. A developer at a large company can evaluate OpenData, ship it inside a product, and send patches back without routing anything through a lawyer. A solo developer can drop it into a side project without thinking about license compatibility. That matters most early on, when the community is small and any friction (a copyleft obligation, a CLA ambiguity, a trademark ask) is enough to stall momentum before it can build.

The adoption a lower-friction license buys us is not a vanity number. It is the raw material for a community flywheel: more users surface edge cases, contributors improve operability and compatibility, integrations pull the system into new stacks, and operators pressure-test the design under load patterns we would never see ourselves. And because OpenData is a family of databases on a shared foundation from day one, the work the community does compounds across the whole family. The kind of diversification MongoDB, Elastic, and Redis had to retrofit into their products under market pressure is already built into the shape of OpenData.

The specific risk people raise most often, a cloud provider running a managed OpenData service, is a success signal more than it is a failure mode. If a hyperscaler decides to host OpenData, the project has already pulled in enough adoption to matter. Our intention over time is to move the OpenData projects into a neutral foundation, so that the license guarantee is backed by governance that outlasts our own company. Being unambiguous about this now is how we earn the trust the flywheel needs to start turning.

## Risks

The risks are real. A large cloud provider could fork OpenData, run it as a managed service, and capture most of the revenue that a thriving community generates. A well-funded competitor could take the code and outspend us on go-to-market. These are the exact scenarios that motivated the license changes at Elastic, Redis, MongoDB, and CockroachDB, and we do not dismiss them.

Our approach to these risks is not to tighten the license but to be honest about what kind of company we are building. A thriving open source database can support many sustainable businesses at once. A larger company can build enterprise extensions or a polished managed developer experience on top of the core. A smaller consultancy can specialize in a vertical and serve a handful of deep customers well. The failure mode is growing the company beyond what the business can actually support, whatever shape the business takes.

The recent history of open source database companies is full of teams that raised large rounds on the assumption of durable high margins, scaled headcount and GTM to match those margins, and had to relicense or restructure when the margins did not hold up. That arc is painful for everyone depending on those companies. The relicense is not really about greed; it reflects a company that grew past what the underlying business could honestly support, and whoever built on the old terms ends up carrying the cost. The only real defense against repeating the pattern is an honest assessment of what we do and do not have. If we keep the team lean for as long as it makes sense, and build a business whose economics do not depend on a moat we do not actually have, we get to take opportunities as they come instead of forcing growth to defend margins we were never entitled to.

We believe the [OpenData](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/manifesto) idea is a genuine contribution to the database space. The MIT license is the most direct path to making that contribution useful to as many people as possible, and building the community is the first step toward making it durable. If you have been watching OpenData and want to contribute, the license is our commitment that your work will stay yours to use.
