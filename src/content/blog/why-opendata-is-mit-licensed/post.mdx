## OpenData and the license question

OpenData is a family of open source databases that share a storage engine built on [SlateDB](http://www.slatedb.io) and object storage. Every database in the family inherits the same operational primitives: storage, durability, tuning surface, cost model. The intent is that once a developer knows how to run one OpenData database, they already know how to run the next. The [project docs](https://www.opendata.dev/docs) and our [manifesto](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/manifesto) cover what we are building and why we think it matters.

We think OpenData can be a substantial contribution to open source databases: a coherent set of database primitives built on a shared, object-store-native foundation, governed in the open and operable by any team that can already run an object store. Every database in the family is MIT-licensed, and the choice was deliberate. When we launched [OpenData Timeseries last week](https://www.opendata.dev/blog/introducing-timeseries), that license prompted two kinds of reactions. Some readers were grateful we had picked a genuinely permissive license. Others were skeptical: why not AGPL, and aren't we worried about a large cloud provider taking the code and running a managed service on top of it?

We picked MIT because it gives OpenData the best shot at mattering. A more restrictive license would not protect us from the pressures every database company faces; it would just slow adoption today. Those concerns are not unfounded, but they overstate the protection a restrictive license actually provides, and they understate the cost of friction in the early years of an open source project. Building a database business is hard no matter which license you pick. The path we are choosing is to be measured about growth, honest about what we do and do not control, and to treat community adoption as the thing actually worth protecting.

## Why open source is the right vehicle for OpenData

Open source is the right vehicle for OpenData for both generic and specific reasons. The generic case is well-understood, so we won't belabor it: more users find more edge cases, contributors close gaps we would not prioritize ourselves, integrations pull the system into stacks we have no line of sight into, and operators pressure-test the design under load patterns we would never see in a startup's test environment. A permissive license removes friction from each of those steps: a developer at a large company can evaluate OpenData, ship it inside a product, and send patches back without routing anything through legal, and a solo developer can drop it into a side project without thinking about compatibility with the rest of their stack. In the early years of a project, the difference between low-friction and high-friction licensing is the difference between adoption starting and adoption stalling.

The specific case is stronger, because OpenData is a family of databases from day one. Every database in the family sits on the same object-store-native foundation, and work the community does on the shared storage engine, the tuning surface, the failure semantics, and the operational model compounds across every database in the family. A contributor fixing a cold-read issue for the timeseries database is also fixing it for the streaming database and the vector database. A user writing an operations runbook for one OpenData system is writing most of the runbook for the next one they adopt. Without a community, building a family of high-quality databases across multiple verticals would take us a decade or two and an engineering organization we do not have. With a community, the horizon shrinks to something we can actually plan for.

Our intention over time is to move the OpenData projects into a neutral foundation, so that the license guarantee is backed by governance that outlasts our own company. No single entity, including ours, should stand between an OpenData user and their right to use the software. Being unambiguous about that intent now is how we start earning the trust a community needs.

## Proprietary database IP does not last

The usual argument for a more restrictive license is protection: against being copied by a competitor, against a cloud provider running a managed service on the code, against the general worry that someone else will extract more value from the project than we will. These are reasonable concerns to raise, and we do not dismiss them. But in the database world, that protection has always been thinner than people believe, and AI coding tools are making it thinner still.

Databases are expensive to build and maintain, but like all software, they are cheap to copy. The novel insight that differentiates a database is usually the thing the vendor markets hardest, because marketing is how the vendor wins deals. Once an insight is described in talks, papers, and docs, re-implementing it is straightforward. What used to take a competitor years now takes weeks with a competent engineer and a coding agent. My co-founder Almog laid this out in more detail in [The Broken Economics of Databases](https://www.bitsxpages.com/p/the-broken-economics-of-databases), but the short version is that every database vendor eventually faces margin pressure from look-alike products. Vendors with an open source core see it earlier and more acutely. Vendors with a proprietary core are not immune; they just get a delay.

This is why so few database companies stay independent over the long run. The ones that did have diversified. Oracle built its sustainable business by moving up the stack into applications (PeopleSoft, Siebel, NetSuite) and down into hardware and cloud infrastructure (Sun, OCI). IBM folded Db2 into a broader software-and-services business and absorbed Informix and Netezza along the way. Sybase ended up inside SAP. Teradata is still independent, but it is not the business it was fifteen years ago. More recently, MongoDB has grown from a document database into a broader platform layering in full-text search, vector search, and stream processing. Elastic has done the same from the opposite direction, starting with search and expanding into observability, security analytics, and vector capabilities. Redis has wrapped its core key-value store in a growing set of modules covering full-text search, JSON, time series, and vector indexing. In every case, the company had to keep adding adjacent database categories to stay viable, because the original database alone was not enough.

Hamilton Helmer's [7 Powers](https://7powers.com/) gives a useful frame here. It describes the properties that let a business generate persistent differential returns: scale economies, network economies, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resources, and process power. Very few database companies have any of them. Steve Ballmer used a similar frame [on Acquired](https://youtu.be/CYC49_aeop0?si=c_JjVNX8ntzH9GPw&t=5388), talking about businesses as zero, one, or two-trick ponies, and observing that almost every business in history is a zero-trick pony. Most database companies, judged honestly, are zero-trick ponies. They carry high fixed costs because database engineers and enterprise sellers are expensive, and they struggle to recoup those costs because competitors erode their edge on a timeline they cannot control. The ones we remember as lasting independent businesses are the ones that used a database as a beachhead for something more defensible.

A restrictive license buys us very little protective upside in the long term, because the ideas behind OpenData will be copied regardless. What it does cost us is adoption, especially early on, when a copyleft obligation or an ambiguous CLA is enough to stall a developer before they have a chance to try the thing. We would rather take the adoption today than the hypothetical protection tomorrow.

## Building a database business in 2026

Given all of that, the real work is in the company we build around the code. Building one that can keep going under the pressures every database company faces has gotten harder, not easier. Open source database companies have been getting monetization wrong for a decade and a half, and AI is compressing the copying cycle that already punished them. Our approach has two parts.

The first is not to assume moats we do not have. Everything we do will be copied, and a cloud hyperscaler could, in principle, take OpenData and run a managed service on top of it. The failure mode most visible in the recent history of this space is not getting copied; it is growing a company that *requires* durable 70-80% gross margins and *requires* compounding growth to keep going. If a business has defensible moats, that shape of growth can work. If it does not and pretends otherwise, the inevitable result is a relicense, an acquisition, or a painful retreat a few years later, and whoever built on the old terms ends up carrying the cost. We would rather keep the team small for as long as that works, so we can take opportunities as they come instead of forcing growth to defend margins we were never entitled to. Modern AI tooling makes this posture more sustainable than it would have been five years ago: a small, senior team can now operate at a scope that used to require several times as many people, which widens the set of business shapes that stay viable without assumed moats.

The second is that OpenData's structure gives us a structural advantage the single-database companies we learn from did not have, and we intend to use it. Because the family shares a storage foundation, cross-database work compounds on both the technical and the business side. Operators adopting one OpenData database have a short path to adopting the next one, because the operational model is already familiar. Tooling, integrations, and operational muscle built around one database carry into the rest. A managed offering over the family gets scope economies that a single-database managed offering cannot. It is too early to commit to specific business shapes, and we are deliberately not doing so before we have earned the right to. The point is that a multi-database footprint changes the base of what is possible in a way single-database predecessors did not have available. The kind of diversification MongoDB, Elastic, and Redis had to retrofit under market pressure is built into the shape of OpenData from the start.

We believe OpenData is a genuine contribution to the database space. MIT is the most direct path to making it useful to as many people as possible, and the community is the first step toward making it durable. If you have been watching OpenData and want to contribute, the license is our commitment that your work will stay yours to use.
